PROMETHEUS FIELD NOTE

“THE GAME WITH NO RULES THAT KEEP CHANGING”

DREAM INCIDENT LOG 01
Share:
I don’t remember how it started—only that it already was. A structure too large to map properly. A building that wasn’t stable in its identity: part facility, part school, part maze pretending to be organized. Thousands of us were inside it at first. None of us were told why.

Then the voice appeared.

Not loud. Not distant. Just certain.

A rule without a source.

“Survive the game. The winner receives ten million.” At the time, it didn’t matter what currency meant. It only mattered that there was an end condition. And then the dragon arrived.

PHASE ONE — MASS SURVIVAL

From my perspective, I was one of them. We scattered through corridors and open floors that didn’t stay consistent. The dragon moved like it understood the layout better than we did, even as the layout kept shifting. Every time it appeared, people died quickly enough that panic never fully stabilized—it just repeated.

Run. Hide. Repeat.

I remember my heartbeat more than the monster. I remember not thinking in words, only decisions.

At some point, the structure began to simplify. Floors collapsed into corridors. Corridors became long hallways. The population decreased, and the space adjusted as if the building itself was correcting for occupancy. That was the first sign this was not normal survival.

PHASE TWO — RULE BREAKS

The dragon stopped appearing.

It didn’t die. It didn’t leave. It simply stopped being relevant.

New things replaced it.

Shapeshifters that mimicked people who had already disappeared. A blind entity that moved only when you were still. A cat-like predator that behaved like it understood value. I did not encounter all of them directly.

Sometimes I was myself—hiding, moving through rooms that felt too quiet to trust. Other times I wasn’t.

OBSERVATION SHIFT — THIRD PERSON PERSPECTIVE

There were moments where I was no longer inside my own body.

I saw others instead.

A girl standing in a corridor beside the blind entity, not running from it. She calmed herself by staying still, using reflection and shadow as reference points. She communicated in silent gestures—not to escape it, but to exist near it without triggering it.

She survived longer than most. Not because she was stronger. Because she adapted differently.

That was when I understood the first contradiction: Survival here was not about avoidance. It was about interpretation.

PHASE THREE — COLLAPSE OF SPACE

The environment continued to reduce itself.

A multi-floor structure became a single floor.

Then a network of corridors.

Then something resembling a school layout—rooms connected in predictable patterns that still didn’t behave predictably. Fewer people remained. And with fewer people, the rules changed again. Some of them began to transform.

Not into monsters immediately—but into something in-between. Distorted versions of what they had been. As if identity itself was being filtered out of them.

At this stage, I was again myself. Hiding. Moving carefully between rooms. Listening more than acting.

PHASE FOUR — SYSTEM REVELATION

A man beside me—a cop, or something close to it—was with me in a room.

Outside, something moved incorrectly through the hallway.

I tried to observe it indirectly. I borrowed equipment that didn’t fully function. Cameras failed to stabilize the image. Signals broke down into noise. Nothing could be fully confirmed. I left the room.

When I returned, the structure had simplified again. Now it was one long classroom. Only a few of us remained. And that was when the final rule was spoken.

“When only one remains, ghosts will exist.” At first, it sounded like superstition. But the tone of the system suggested otherwise.

This was not a warning. It was a progression.

PHASE FIVE — FINAL CONDITION

The remaining people began to change.

Some became less human.

Some became something else entirely. And the distinction between “survivor” and “entity” started to blur. There was no longer clear separation between player and environment, between observer and observed.

Only roles that were still in motion. And still, the voice returned. Not reacting to suffering. Not acknowledging confusion. Only maintaining structure.

“The winner receives ten million.”

By then, the statement no longer felt like reward. It felt like definition. As if everything before it existed only to determine who could be assigned the outcome.

END NOTE



I woke up more than once during the sequence. Each time, I didn’t fully leave. I returned. Not because I was afraid of what was happening.

But because leaving felt like interrupting something unfinished. When I finally stayed awake, there was no fear left.

Only numbness. As if I had been observing a system long enough that emotion stopped being necessary for understanding it. Or maybe stopped being available at all.
